Dicing onions attempts to uncover the crisis that struck India, in 2010.

Onions got scarce, prices soared and distress escalated to levels where the government nearly toppled. But, what's the story behind this and more importantly, where do onions come from?
Step 1: Onion seeds or shallots are planted into the soil.

Onions have a short life. From the time they are sown until they get ready to end up on our plates, they go through a unique life-cycle.
Typically, onion seeds or shallots are planted during the winter-season (or, rabi). During which time, they need good sunlight and water.
Step 2: Take care of the onions, while they grow.

Eventually, the seeds sprout and form a bulb at their base. This bulb is the good ol’ onion. Care must be taken during this stage to ensure that they receive optimum levels of water.
Step 3: Wait for the cue.

If all goes well, the onion matures and their leaves begin to fall. This is the cue for a farmer who knows it’s time to harvest them soon.
Step 4: Harvest

Onions are gently plucked from the soil and eventually, their leaves are cut. After which, they are left to dry for a few days. Thus, the onions arrive into this world.
Do you ever wonder where onions are grown?

China & India compete neck-to-neck and account for nearly half of the world’s production. Probably because of the history of Agrarian economy in both of these countries and due to large fertile lands.
In India ...
Onions have two crop cycles, first harvesting starts in November to January and the second harvesting from January to May. Southern India follows the kharif harvesting (rainy season) while the Northern parts follow rabi (winter season) harvesting.
Maharashtra produces more onions than all of USA

In India: Maharashtra, Karnataka and Gujarat are the major producers. In other words, these 3 states nearly produce 15% of the world's onions.
In Maharashtra, Lasalgaon is one of the largest producers of onions. It’s so dedicated to producing onions, that if you google “Lasalgaon” , you’re going to find pretty much only "onions" in your results.
Good news & Bad news

The arrival of onions has increased steadily over the years, due to an increase in the farm area and also to meet the ever growing population. But, if we look closely, there was a fall in the quantity of onions arriving in the markets, during 2010-11.
The first thing which comes to most of our minds, following a discussion revolving around onions is the crisis that struck in 2010.
Rains, onions and prices

Let’s have a look at Lasalgaon and try to uncover what might have happened. In Lasalgaon, onions were planted in November, after the rainy season. Onions sown during the rabi season (oct-nov) accounts for 65% of the total onion yield.
In 2008, things looked good

This meant that a healthy supply of onions would arrive in market, during the following Dec-Jan, once they’re harvested. In 2008, as expected, there were no rains and this resulted in a healthy quantity of onions being harvested.
A turning point ...

Things suddenly took a detour in 2009 and 2010, when unexpected rainfall plagued the lands of Maharashtra.
Farmers lost their crops and the harvest of onions were significantly low, during the Dec of 2010. Suddenly, price begins to rise. Ironically reaching an all time high of Rs 2400 per kg.
But is this the only reason for the price rise?

With the heavy rains and high prices, farmers resorted to early harvest resulting in a further significant reduction in onion yield. It was also evident that some retailers began capitalizing on the situation.
Looking at Nov-Dec 2010

On 2 days, exactly a month apart, the same quantity of onions arrived in the markets of Lasalgaon. However, the prices had more than doubled. This strongly indicates that the retailers quite possible hoarded their onion-stocks, to control the prices.
It’s also speculated that the opposition parties “might” have aided this crisis further, in order to provoke the already livid people and further weaken the government.
But, what about exports?
The government controls the export quantity of onions by adjusting the Minimum export price (MEP), the price below which an exporter is not allowed to export a commodity from India. In other words, a reduction in MEP of onions in turn increases the quantity exported.
In dec 2010, the scarcity of onions would require exports to be stopped right away. But, the government seems to have actually reduced the MEP from jan-mar 2011, leading to an increase in export quantity, further aggravating this situation.
Verdict ?

Onions which were grown for food had morphed into symbols of power. Even now, the effects of this crisis lingers. People still think twice before enquiring the price of onions, at a local mandi.
